## Summary
In this interview with Yegor Bugayenko, Vladimir Khorikov, author of "Unit Testing Principles, Practices, and Patterns," discusses the fundamental necessity of automated testing for software developers. The core argument Khorikov presents is that tests are not primarily about catching bugs in the short term, but about maintaining development speed over the lifetime of a project. 

He explains that at the beginning of a project, development speed is naturally high because there is no legacy code or technical debt. However, as the codebase grows, entropy increases, and original architectural decisions may no longer fit new requirements. This leads to the accumulation of "technical debt" and "crutches" (hacks) that slow down the team. To counteract this, constant refactoring is required. Khorikov defines unit tests as a "safety cushion" that allows developers to refactor and evolve the architecture confidently. Without this cushion, the fear of breaking existing functionality prevents necessary improvements, leading to a terminal slowdown.

Furthermore, Khorikov addresses the challenge of scaling. As a project's complexity grows exponentially, it eventually exceeds the capacity of a human developer to hold the entire system in their mind. At this stage, manual testing becomes a bottleneck and a source of constant regressions. Automated tests serve as an external memory and verification tool that replaces inefficient manual processes, ensuring that the project remains manageable and sustainable as it scales.

## Key Insights
- "Tests serve as a safety cushion that allows you to refactor and change your architecture to meet new requirements without slowing down."
- "The primary reason to write tests is to maintain the speed of development over time by managing technical debt and code entropy."
- "As project complexity grows exponentially, it eventually exceeds your mental capacity to track it; tests provide the necessary support to manage that complexity."
- "Unit testing is essentially a tool to replace manual regression testing with automation, allowing for more frequent and reliable releases."

## Relevance: 5/5
This discussion is highly relevant to professional software engineering, focusing on long-term sustainability, technical debt management, and the economic justification for quality practices.
