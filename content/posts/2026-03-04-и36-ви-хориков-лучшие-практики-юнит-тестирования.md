## Summary
In this interview, Vladimir Khorikov, author of "Unit Testing Principles, Practices, and Patterns," discusses the fundamental necessity of automated testing. He argues that the primary motivation for writing tests is not merely to find bugs, but to maintain development speed throughout a project's lifecycle. At the beginning of a project, progress is fast because the codebase is small and easy to change. However, as the project grows, "entropy" and technical debt inevitably accumulate. 

Without continuous refactoring, this technical debt leads to "crutches" in the code, making new features harder to implement. Tests serve as a vital "safety cushion" that allows developers to refactor and evolve the architecture confidently. Khorikov notes that project complexity often grows exponentially, eventually exceeding a developer's mental capacity to remember every detail. Automated tests replace manual verification, reducing regression bugs and ensuring that the cost of change remains manageable as the system evolves.

## Key Insights
1. "The most important reason to write tests is to preserve development speed over time."
2. "Tests act as a safety cushion, enabling the constant refactoring necessary to combat technical debt."
3. "As project complexity grows exponentially, it eventually surpasses the human brain's ability to hold the entire system in memory."
