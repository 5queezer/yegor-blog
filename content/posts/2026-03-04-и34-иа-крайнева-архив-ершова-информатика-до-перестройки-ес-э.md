## Summary
In this interview, historian Irina Krayneva discusses her work on the monumental archive of Academician Andrei Ershov, a pioneer of Soviet informatics. Despite not being a programmer herself, Krayneva spent seven years lead-authoring Ershov's scientific biography and digitizing his legacy at the Institute of Informatics Systems in Novosibirsk. The archive is extraordinarily detailed, containing over 500 folders—significantly more than the official institutional records of the entire Computing Center. It includes everything from professional correspondence and manuscripts to mundane items like Aeroflot baggage receipts, reflecting Ershov's lifelong habit of meticulous documentation.

The project began in the mid-1990s as the internet reached Akademgorodok. It was initially sparked by a suggestion from Igor Agamirzyan (then representing Microsoft) and funded by a Microsoft grant. The work was a multidisciplinary collaboration: programmers developed the information system while historians scanned, categorized, and described the documents. Krayneva explains that at the time, the "history of informatics" barely existed as a field. The ultimate goal of preserving such a legacy is not just archival; it is about studying past experiences to find practical applications and insights for modern science. The interview highlights the shift from viewing computer science purely as a technical tool to treating it as a historical subject worthy of rigorous academic study.

## Key Insights
* "Science is a real way to satisfy one's curiosity at the state's expense." (Quoting Lev Artsimovich)
* Ershov’s personal archive (500+ folders) dwarfed the official institutional archive (70 folders), proving that history is often preserved by individuals rather than organizations.
* The digitization of Ershov's legacy was a pioneering 7-year interdisciplinary effort that bridged the gap between historical research and software development.
* Studying the "Programmer's Path" helps identify how early computational challenges and organizational structures still influence modern practices.
