This transcript features an interview between **Yegor Bugayenko** and **Irina Kraineva**, a historian at the Ershov Institute of Informatics Systems. The discussion centers on the life and legacy of **Andrey Ershov**, a pioneer of Soviet computer science, and the massive effort required to preserve his personal archives.

### ## Summary

The conversation explores the intersection of history and computer science through the lens of Academician Andrey Ershov’s "Programmer’s Path." Irina Kraineva, though not a programmer herself, spent over seven years digitizing Ershov’s colossal archive—a collection of over 500 files that dwarfs the official records of the very institute he founded. This archive is remarkable for its granularity, containing everything from high-level scientific correspondence and manuscripts to mundane Aeroflot baggage receipts, reflecting Ershov’s lifelong commitment to documenting the evolution of Soviet informatics.

A significant portion of the discussion focuses on the "archaeology" of Soviet computing. In the mid-1990s, following the collapse of the USSR, the Russian scientific community faced a crisis of identity and funding. This sparked a "reflexive" movement to document their past achievements, leading to the creation of the electronic Ershov archive. Interestingly, the project was jump-started by a grant from **Microsoft** in the late 1990s, highlighting a period of international cooperation where Western tech giants helped preserve the history of their former Cold War rivals.

Kraineva touches upon the "theory of missed opportunities," a sentiment prevalent among the Soviet engineering community. There is a deep-seated regret regarding the trajectory of Soviet hardware and software development—specifically the shift from original domestic designs to the cloning of Western systems (like the IBM-inspired ES EVM mentioned in the title). The interview suggests that by studying these archives, modern engineers can understand the "missed chances" where Soviet innovation was either stifled by bureaucracy or abandoned in favor of "import substitution" (or rather, "import cloning"). Ultimately, the work serves as both a scientific biography and a cautionary historical roadmap for the development of complex technological systems under state pressure.
