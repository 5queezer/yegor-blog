## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko discusses the rapid evolution of AI-generated filmmaking with Pavel Peregudov, organizer of the MyFilm48 festival. The conversation centers on the transition from simple image generation to the complex craft of narrative cinema. While Yegor observes that generating a single AI frame seems trivial, Pavel clarifies that creating a cohesive film requires rigorous control over "consistency"—maintaining the same character appearance, lighting, and environment across multiple shots. 

A significant portion of the discussion addresses the technical limitations currently defining the "AI aesthetic," such as the prevalence of very short clips (0.5 to 5 seconds). Pavel explains that while these were once hard limits of neural networks, newer models are allowing for iterative frame extension, though maintaining character integrity over long durations remains a "technical barrier" being solved in real-time. 

Perhaps the most striking insight is the speed of development; Pavel notes that films produced just two months apart show "colossal" differences in quality due to the rapid release cycles of core AI models. This creates a new professional class: the "AI Generalist." These individuals are not traditional cinematographers but experts at steering generative tools. Ultimately, both agree that while the barrier to entry is lowering, the fundamental value of cinema remains the story. Just as a masterpiece can be shot on a phone or a failure on an IMAX camera, AI is merely a tool that requires human vision and skill to produce meaningful art.

## Key Insights
* "Technically, generating a frame takes two words and one button, but a film is not a single frame; it requires depth, meaning, and consistency."
* "The difference in quality between films made even two months apart is colossal because neural networks and human skills are evolving at a breakneck pace."
* "We are seeing the birth of 'AI Generalists'—professionals who don't just shoot film, but master the iterative process of generating it."
* "Regardless of the technology used, any film is, first and foremost, a story."

## Relevance: 3/5
The discussion parallels software engineering in its focus on rapid tool evolution, the shift from manual labor to "generative" workflows, and the necessity of maintaining architectural consistency (state) across a complex system. It highlights how senior expertise shifts from "how to build" to "what to build" as tools become more powerful.
