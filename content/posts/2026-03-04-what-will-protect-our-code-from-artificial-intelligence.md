## Summary
In this talk, Yegor Bugayenko presents an unconventional perspective on Artificial Intelligence in software engineering. Rather than fearing job displacement, he expresses concern that AI will become a ubiquitous "co-pilot" that amplifies existing bad habits in the industry. To prepare for this future, Bugayenko revisits three fundamental "annoyances" of programming: defects, security vulnerabilities, and complexity.

He argues that bugs are an inevitable byproduct of development, citing Steve McConnell’s classic metric of 10-20 errors per 1,000 lines of code. Bugayenko suggests that developers should stop viewing bugs as failures and instead treat them as "fuel" for the development process—the more bugs a team finds and logs, the higher the quality of the final product. Similarly, security holes (like SQL injection) are presented as unavoidable risks that require constant vigilance rather than perfectionist denial.

The core of his argument centers on complexity and "dirty code." He asserts that the true mark of a senior engineer is the ability to write simple, readable code that even a junior developer can grasp. He warns that complex, convoluted code is a sign of a poor programmer. While the transcript truncates before the full "protection" strategy is revealed, the implication is clear: to survive and thrive alongside AI, programmers must focus on radical simplicity and strict quality metrics. If we provide AI with clean, simple, and highly tested patterns, it becomes a tool; if we feed it complexity and "clever" code, it becomes a source of technical debt.

## Key Insights
- "I'm afraid of AI... not because it will fire me, but because it will sit next to me and program."
- "Bugs are the fuel that the development team runs on; the more you find, the fewer the users will encounter."
- "If you think complex code is an achievement, you misunderstand your profession; complex code is the sign of a bad programmer."
- "A good programmer writes simple code that is easily understood by colleagues and even junior developers."
