## Summary
This interview features Andrey Borodin, a prominent PostgreSQL contributor, in conversation with Yegor Bugayenko. The discussion opens with a lighthearted debate over the "correct" pronunciation of PostgreSQL, where Andrey argues that as long as engineers understand each other without misinterpretation, the exact phonetics are secondary to technical clarity. Andrey traces his journey with the database, moving from a user in 2007 to writing his first patch in 2012. 

A core theme of the conversation is the comparison between PostgreSQL and MySQL. Andrey posits that PostgreSQL’s architectural openness and its status as a "truly free" database allowed it to outpace MySQL in terms of community engagement. He notes that contributing to MySQL today is difficult without being an Oracle employee, whereas PostgreSQL offers a democratic platform for anyone to "inflict utility." 

The dialogue then shifts to the infamously "prehistoric" development process of PostgreSQL, which relies on mailing lists rather than modern platforms like GitHub. While Yegor criticizes the lack of syntax highlighting and the fragmented nature of email threads, Andrey defends the system. He describes himself as a "boiled frog" who has grown to appreciate the focus on human thought and logical substance over superficial UI features. He highlights that reading these professional exchanges is not just about code, but about learning how top-tier developers structure their reasoning and even improving one's technical English through the nuanced language used by senior maintainers.

## Key Insights
- "Engineers should prioritize mutual understanding; whether it is 'Postgres' or 'Postgree' is irrelevant as long as the system components are not confused."
- "PostgreSQL is a truly free database where an individual can bring significant value to the core without needing to work for a corporate giant like Oracle."
- "Tools are rarely the actual problem in software development; the focus must remain on the depth and logic of the thought process, not just the aesthetic of the code."
- "The mailing list acts as a window into the minds of experts, showing not just what they write, but how they structure their logic and arguments."

## Relevance: 5/5
Extremely relevant for understanding the cultural divide between modern "GitHub-first" development and the rigorous, thought-oriented traditions of major open-source infrastructure projects.
