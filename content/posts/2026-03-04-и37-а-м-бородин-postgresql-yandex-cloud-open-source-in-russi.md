## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko talks with Andrey Borodin, a long-time contributor to the PostgreSQL project and an engineer at Yandex Cloud. The discussion begins with a lighthearted debate over the "correct" pronunciation of PostgreSQL, where Borodin emphasizes that for engineers, clear communication is more important than linguistic purity. Borodin shares his journey with the database, having started as a user in 2007 and writing his first patch in 2012.

A significant portion of the conversation focuses on the comparison between PostgreSQL and MySQL. While Borodin acknowledges that both are excellent tools, he highlights PostgreSQL's status as a truly "free" and open-source database. He notes that contributing to MySQL today is difficult without being an Oracle employee, whereas PostgreSQL’s community-driven model allows for more direct impact. 

The most provocative part of the dialogue centers on the PostgreSQL development process, which still relies heavily on mailing lists (PGSQL Hackers). Yegor describes this as "prehistoric" and chaotic, citing the lack of syntax highlighting and the difficulty of tracking threads. Borodin defends this "old-school" approach, arguing that the project matured before modern tools like Git or GitHub existed. He posits that while tools are important, they are rarely the bottleneck. He makes a profound point that while modern IDEs can highlight code colors, they cannot highlight "human thoughts," which are the most critical part of a contribution. To him, code is "shallow," while the logic, intent, and architectural meaning conveyed in the accompanying letters are what truly matter.

## Key Insights
- "Engineers speak so that they understand each other... terms should not be mixed, but if you are understood without misinterpretation, call it whatever you want."
- "Code is a shallow thing that anyone can produce. It is more important that it makes sense in its meaning and logic."
- "We haven't learned to highlight human thoughts yet [like we highlight syntax]. The most interesting part is the intent and the letter."
- "Tools are almost never the problem; the development of Postgres flourished long before modern version control systems became standard."

## Relevance: 4/5
High relevance for software engineers interested in the philosophy of open-source maintenance, the trade-offs between modern DX (Developer Experience) and rigorous logical review, and the history of one of the world's most successful community-led projects.
