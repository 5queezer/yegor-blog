## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko talks with Pavel Peregudov, organizer of the MyFilm48 festival, about the rapidly evolving world of AI-generated cinema. While a single frame can be generated with just a few words, Peregudov explains that creating a coherent film is a complex and "tedious" process. The primary challenge lies in maintaining consistency—ensuring that characters' appearances, backgrounds, lighting, and clothing do not "drift" or change unpredictably between shots. He compares AI tools to cameras: just as a professional camera doesn't guarantee a good movie, high-end AI tools are only as effective as the person using them.

The discussion highlights the technical limitations that have historically forced AI films to consist of very short, choppy clips. However, newer models are beginning to allow for longer iterations and smoother transitions. Peregudov notes that the pace of development is staggering; the quality gap between tools released only two months apart can be massive. This has led to the rise of a new professional role: the "AI Generalist." These individuals don't just "prompt"; they master the iterative process of controlling generative chaos to achieve a specific artistic vision. Ultimately, Peregudov emphasizes that despite the technological leaps, a film remains primarily a story. The tools are secondary to the narrative depth and the creator's intent.

## Key Insights
- "Technically, it's easy to generate a frame, but a film isn't just one frame; it requires controlling consistency in appearance, lighting, and meaning."
- "The barriers are being solved in leaps and bounds; the difference in quality even over a two-month period is colossal."
- "AI filmmaking is giving rise to 'AI Generalists'—people who learn to master these tools through constant practice and trial and error."
- "Regardless of how well you generate, any film or video is, first and foremost, a story."

## Relevance: 3/5
The discussion is highly relevant to management and workflow optimization. It parallels software engineering in its treatment of rapid tool evolution, the necessity of maintaining "state" (consistency) across a system, and the emergence of new technical specialties. It also touches on the "human-in-the-loop" requirement for quality assurance in automated processes.
