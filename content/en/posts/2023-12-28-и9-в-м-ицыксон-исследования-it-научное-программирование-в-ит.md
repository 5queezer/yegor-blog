## Summary
This interview between Yegor Bugayenko and Vladimir Itzykson explores the intersection of academic research and practical software engineering at ITMO University. Itzykson highlights a unique challenge in IT research: the difficulty of making truly "fundamental" discoveries. Since the field is intensely applied, many innovations often exist in existing codebases before they are ever formally described in scientific papers. This makes comprehensive literature reviews particularly difficult, as researchers often find that their "new" ideas have already been implemented somewhere in the industry without being documented as science.

A major theme of the discussion is the potential "vocationalization" of higher education. Itzykson warns against universities turning into advanced trade schools that merely train students for specific tech stacks demanded by big tech companies. Instead, he advocates for a balance where students learn the scientific method—formulating research questions, comparing methodologies, and rigorously justifying their technical choices. At ITMO, the focus is on building tools for software development and analysis, rather than just being a consumer of them.

The conversation also touches on the motivations of researchers. While industry offers significantly higher salaries, academia provides creative freedom and the opportunity to work with elite students on long-term problems without immediate commercial pressure. Itzykson emphasizes that the primary goal of scientific training for engineers is to enable them to provide provable, justified solutions to complex problems, distinguishing high-level architects from simple coders.

## Key Insights
- "Everything you've come up with is likely already in the code somewhere; the challenge is finding and formalizing it."
- "A university shouldn't turn into an advanced vocational school just to satisfy immediate corporate hiring needs."
- "A scientific result in IT is often the ability to rigorously justify why a specific solution is superior to others, not just making it work."
- "Academic freedom and the ability to choose your own problems are the primary reasons top researchers stay in universities despite the pay gap."

## Relevance: 4/5
High relevance for software engineers interested in the transition from coding to system design and for those considering the value of a master's or PhD in a practical field.
