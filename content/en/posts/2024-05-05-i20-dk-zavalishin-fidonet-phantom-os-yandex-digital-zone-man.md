## Summary
In this interview, Dmitry Zavalishin, a cornerstone of the Russian IT industry, discusses his journey from the early days of Fidonet to founding Digital Zone and creating Phantom OS. The conversation begins with the cultural significance of Fidonet, which Zavalishin describes as a "network of friends" that established the professional and ethical foundations for Russian developers before the Internet became accessible. 

He reflects on his tenure at Yandex, where he played a pivotal role in launching Yandex.Market. Zavalishin contrasts the experience of managing large-scale corporate departments with the creative freedom found in private entrepreneurship. He argues that while corporations offer massive impact, private companies allow for more radical technical experimentation. 

A significant portion of the discussion is dedicated to Phantom OS—a revolutionary persistent operating system. Unlike traditional systems that rely on files, Phantom OS treats the entire environment as a collection of long-lived objects. This architecture eliminates the need for "saving" data, as the system state is intrinsically persistent. Finally, Zavalishin shares his management philosophy, emphasizing that true professionalism is the ability to maintain quality and discipline even when tasks are uninteresting, and highlighting the "human element" as the most complex variable in any IT project.

## Key Insights
- **Fidonet's Legacy:** Fidonet was not just a network but a school of discipline and communication that shaped the first generation of elite post-Soviet engineers.
- **Phantom OS Persistence:** Traditional operating systems are flawed because they force a "file-centric" view; a truly object-oriented OS should be persistent, where objects never "die" unless explicitly deleted.
- **The Scale vs. Freedom Trade-off:** Big tech (like Yandex) provides the tools for massive reach, but smaller private firms (like Digital Zone) are the true incubators for architectural breakthroughs.
- **Definition of Professionalism:** Professionalism in software engineering is the capacity to deliver results consistently, regardless of personal motivation or the "boring" nature of the work.

## Relevance: 5/5
This interview is highly relevant for software architects and managers, as it bridges deep OS-level theory (persistence, OOP) with practical high-level management and the historical evolution of networking.
