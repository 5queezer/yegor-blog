## Summary
In this intense interview, Yegor Bugayenko sits down with Anatoly Shalyto, a legendary professor at ITMO University and a pioneer of automata-based programming. The conversation centers on the "ITMO phenomenon"—how a single university became a global powerhouse in competitive programming (ICPC). Shalyto explains that their success isn't accidental but the result of a cultivated environment of "healthy competitiveness" and the presence of world-class mentors like Andrey Stankevich. 

A major point of contention is the purpose of higher education. While Bugayenko often advocates for universities as centers of pure science, Shalyto takes a fiercely pragmatic stance. He views the university as a high-end "factory" for elite engineering talent. He argues that a student’s coursework should be so polished and professional that it serves as their primary resume for companies like Yandex or JetBrains. Shalyto also discusses his "IT stand-up" teaching style, which he uses to keep young students engaged while addressing the challenges of teaching the "people's militia"—the mass of students entering IT solely for the high salaries rather than a genuine calling. Finally, they touch on Shalyto's technical passion: automata-based programming, which he champions as a way to make complex logic transparent and maintainable.

## Key Insights
*   "A student's coursework should be so good that they can show it to their future boss instead of an interview."
*   "The 'People's Militia' in IT consists of those who came for the money; our task is to turn the best of them into craftsmen."
*   "A professor stays relevant by being an 'IT stand-up' artist—keeping students' attention is half the battle."
*   "Automata-based programming is the key to making complex software logic readable and verifiable."
