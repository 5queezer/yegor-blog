## Summary
In this lecture, Yegor Bugayenko strips away the "soft skills" facade of communication management, treating it instead as a technical resource that incurs significant overhead. The core of the session revolves around the mathematical reality of communication channels: the formula $n(n-1)/2$. Yegor explains that as team size ($n$) increases, the number of potential communication paths grows quadratically. Without strict management, a project will eventually collapse under the weight of its own "noise"—unstructured, redundant, or irrelevant information exchange.

He categorizes communication into three types: Interactive (meetings, calls), Push (emails, newsletters), and Pull (wikis, dashboards, ticket systems). Yegor strongly advocates for "Pull" communication because it is persistent, traceable, and minimizes interruptions. He argues that the primary goal of a Project Manager (PM) is not to encourage more talking, but to act as a filter that reduces noise and enforces formal, written communication. According to his philosophy, if it isn't written down in a tracking system, it didn't happen. Status reporting should be objective and automated where possible, rather than based on subjective "feelings" shared in long meetings.

## Key Insights
- "Communication is not a benefit; it is an overhead. Your goal as a PM is to minimize it, not maximize it."
- "The formula $n(n-1)/2$ is the most dangerous equation in project management. It explains why large teams often produce less than small ones."
- "Oral communication is noise. It leaves no trace and creates ambiguity. If you want results, use tickets and pull-based documentation."
- "A PM is a noise reducer. If you are just a relay for messages, you are part of the noise."

## Relevance: 5/5
Extremely relevant for software engineering. The insights on channel overhead directly mirror Brooks's Law and are essential for scaling development teams and maintaining codebase integrity through formal traceability (tickets/PRs).
