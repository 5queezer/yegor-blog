## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko speaks with Denis Dimitrov, a key researcher and leader at Sber AI, about the development of Russia's leading generative AI models: Malevich, Kandinsky, and GigaChat. A major focal point of the discussion is Dimitrov's claim that his team was actually the first in the world to release a public text-to-image model in early November 2021. This early iteration, part of the Malevich project, predated the widely known public launches of Stable Diffusion and Midjourney.

The conversation deeply explores the intersection of artificial intelligence research and practical software engineering. Dimitrov emphasizes the critical difference between the two disciplines. He explains how these groundbreaking projects initially started as "disruptive research" initiatives within Sber AI, undertaken without direct top-down orders from management. While researchers prove initial concepts, it requires a robust, scalable software engineering team to transform those theoretical ideas into stable, flagship products that millions can use. He notes that despite global competition, Sber AI remains focused on building fully independent models trained from scratch on their own supercomputers (like Christofari), rather than simply replicating Western technologies.

Additionally, the interview delves into team management within deep tech environments. Dimitrov shares his philosophy that in the highly complex field of AI research, experiencing failure or lack of progress for months is a completely normal and expected part of the discovery process. He argues that developers and researchers are inherently most productive when they are given the freedom to tackle tasks they are genuinely passionate about. This bottom-up approach to innovation provides an interesting contrast to the more rigid, traditional management theories often discussed by Bugayenko.

## Key Insights
- "We released the first public text-to-image model in November 2021, before Stable Diffusion and Midjourney."
- "These flagship models started as disruptive, bottom-up research initiatives without direct management orders."
- "There is a massive gap between proving a concept in a research paper and engineering a scalable, production-ready system."
- "In complex AI research, failing for a month or two is not a disaster; it is a normal part of the discovery process."
