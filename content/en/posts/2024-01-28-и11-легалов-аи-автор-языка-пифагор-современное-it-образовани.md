## Summary
The interview features Alexander Legalov, the creator of the dataflow programming language "Pythagoras", discussing the current state and future trajectory of IT education in the Russian Federation, specifically drawing from his experiences at the Higher School of Economics (HSE). The conversation delves into the core challenges of modern computer science pedagogy, emphasizing the friction between teaching enduring computer science fundamentals versus catering to the industry's demand for immediate proficiency in trendy, short-lived frameworks. Legalov elaborates on the design philosophy behind his language, Pythagoras, highlighting how functional and dataflow programming paradigms offer elegant solutions to complex architectural problems that traditional Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) might overcomplicate. By focusing on dataflow, he argues that students and engineers can develop a more robust mental model for parallel processing and system design. Furthermore, the discussion touches upon the systemic issues in university curricula, noting a significant gap between academic theory and the practical realities of commercial software engineering. Legalov advocates for a paradigm shift in education—one that prioritizes teaching students how to think critically and architecturally over rote memorization of syntax. Ultimately, the interview serves as a profound reflection on how we cultivate the next generation of software engineers, making it a valuable resource for tech leads, educators, and developers interested in language design and team mentorship.

## Key Insights
1. "Teaching fundamental programming paradigms is far more valuable than training students on the latest transient frameworks."
2. "Dataflow and functional concepts often solve architectural complexities that traditional OOP paradigms struggle with or overcomplicate."
3. "There is a systemic gap between academic theory and the practical engineering realities of the commercial tech industry."
4. "Our goal in IT education must be to teach students how to think critically and architecturally about software design."
