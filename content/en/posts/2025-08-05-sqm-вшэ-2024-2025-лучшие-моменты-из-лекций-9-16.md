## Summary
This lecture series by Yegor Bugayenko at the Higher School of Economics (HSE) focuses on Software Quality Metrics (SQM), providing a rigorous mathematical and architectural approach to code health. Lectures 9 through 16 transition from low-level cohesion metrics to high-level process management and technical debt mitigation.

The technical core begins with an analysis of class cohesion using metrics like CAMC and NHD, emphasizing that focused classes are easier to maintain. Bugayenko then introduces the MOOD (Metrics for Object-Oriented Design) framework to quantify encapsulation, inheritance, and polymorphism across entire projects. He argues that these metrics are not just "nice to have" but essential for objective quality control.

The latter half of the series tackles "code rot." Bugayenko discusses the "sin" of code duplication and the dangers of "Dead Code," where developers' fear of breaking systems leads to a buildup of useless instructions that increase cognitive load. A significant portion is dedicated to Source Code Volatility and Code Churn, demonstrating a direct correlation between high change frequency and defect density. Finally, the series introduces "Puzzle Driven Development" (PDD) as a systematic way to manage "Self-Admitted Technical Debt" (SATD). Instead of ignoring small issues or creating messy "quick fixes," PDD mandates converting incomplete tasks into tracked "puzzles," ensuring code remains clean without blocking immediate delivery.

## Key Insights
- "The monolith is a graveyard for quality; manyrepos provide the isolation necessary for strict metric enforcement."
- "Dead code is not just harmless bytes; it is a cognitive tax that developers pay every time they read a file they are afraid to touch."
- "Puzzle Driven Development (PDD) turns technical debt from a hidden liability into a managed, automated workflow."
- "Code Churn is a leading indicator of instability; a spike in changes at the end of a cycle is a signal of imminent failure."

## Relevance: 5/5
These lectures are highly relevant for senior engineers and architects, as they bridge the gap between abstract "clean code" principles and measurable engineering discipline.
