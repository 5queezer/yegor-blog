## Summary
In this deep-dive interview, Yegor Bugayenko converses with Alexander Nedorya, a pivotal figure in the Soviet and Russian software engineering landscape, particularly known for his work in Novosibirsk's Akademgorodok. The discussion traces the historical roots of the legendary Kronos workstation project, an ambitious Soviet endeavor to build a 32-bit computer heavily inspired by Niklaus Wirth’s Lilith architecture and the Modula-2 programming language. Nedorya reflects on Wirth's profound influence on their engineering culture, especially following Wirth's personal visits to the USSR. 

A central theme of the conversation is the concept of "Architectural Programming," a disciplined methodology that prioritizes system-level design, rigorous modularity, and inherent simplicity over hasty code generation. Nedorya shares his extensive experience transitioning from building world-class, highly optimized compilers (with the Excelsior team) to navigating modern software engineering challenges at Trivil. He draws a sharp contrast between the meticulous, architecturally sound engineering required to build operating systems or compilers from scratch, and the sometimes chaotic, fast-paced nature of modern Agile software development. By focusing on strong typing, clear interfaces, and deep architectural integrity, Nedorya argues that developers can manage software complexity much more effectively, echoing Wirth's timeless principles in today's cloud-native and OOP-dominated world. Ultimately, this interview serves as a bridge connecting foundational computer science history with contemporary software architecture challenges, offering invaluable lessons on how to cultivate engineering excellence within teams.

## Key Insights
- Wirth's philosophy dictates that true elegance in code is achieved through deliberate simplicity and strict modularity, not by expanding the codebase.
- The process of building a compiler is the ultimate school for software discipline, forcing engineers to account for all edge cases and structural dependencies.
- The Kronos project serves as a historical testament to what a small, highly cohesive team can build from scratch when grounded in a strong theoretical foundation.
- "Architectural programming" means that the structural integrity of a system and the contracts between its modules must be fully resolved before implementation begins.
