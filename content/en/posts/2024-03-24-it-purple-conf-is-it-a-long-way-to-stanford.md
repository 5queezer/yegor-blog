## Summary
In this provocative talk at the IT Purple Conf, Yegor Bugayenko addresses the significant disparity between the success of Russian programmers in competitive coding (like ICPC) and their nearly non-existent contribution to global Computer Science research. He argues that while the industry produces excellent "craftsmen" or "coders," it fails to cultivate "research engineers" who contribute to the fundamental advancement of the field. Bugayenko points out that top-tier universities like Stanford or MIT are defined not by the number of lines of code their students write, but by their scientific output, citations, and presence in prestigious journals (ACM/IEEE).

He proposes a five-step plan to bridge this gap: integrating scientific publishing into the university curriculum, providing institutional support for academic writing in English, establishing "Paper Reading" clubs to study foundational and modern research, shifting competition focus from speed-coding to architectural innovation, and fostering international-level scientific conferences within the local ecosystem. The ultimate goal is to transform software engineering from a purely vocational skill into a rigorous scientific discipline, ensuring long-term professional relevance and global impact.

## Key Insights
- "We have plenty of programmers, but almost no Computer Science researchers."
- "Winning coding competitions is a dead end; real prestige comes from Turing Awards and highly-cited papers."
- "A published scientific paper is a more permanent and impactful legacy than a thousand lines of industrial code."
- "To reach Stanford's level, students must stop just 'coding' and start participating in the global academic dialogue."

## Relevance: 4/5
The talk is highly relevant to software engineering as it challenges the "craftsman" mindset and promotes a more disciplined, research-oriented approach to building systems and understanding architecture.
