## Summary
In this introductory lecture of the PMBA series, Yegor Bugayenko defines the core identity of a Project Manager (PM) through the lens of Integration Management. He argues that a project is a temporary endeavor with a definitive beginning and end, delivering a tangible artifact. Contrary to popular belief, Yegor asserts that the PM’s primary objective is not to "keep the project going," but to **close it** as quickly and accurately as possible within the predefined boundaries of the "Triple Constraint": Scope, Time, and Budget.

The PM acts as a vital buffer and controller between conflicting interests. Customers naturally push for scope creep, while developers often seek more time for perfection or higher budgets. Integration Management serves as the "glue" that binds all project processes—Charter, Management Plan, Execution, Monitoring, Change Control, and Closing—into a cohesive whole. Yegor also redefines project success, moving beyond simple "customer happiness" to a balanced state where the interests of both positive and negative stakeholders are managed. He advocates for a "hard" management style based on strict rules and tracking, rather than relying solely on intrinsic motivation.

## Key Insights
- "The Project Manager is the only person in the project whose primary interest is to stop the project."
- "A stakeholder is anyone who can influence the project or is influenced by it; often, the most important ones are those who will suffer if the project succeeds."
- "Integration Management is not about doing the work; it's about making sure the work is coordinated so the project can actually end."
- "The Triple Constraint (Scope, Time, Budget) is a law, not a suggestion. If you change one, you must change the others."

## Relevance: 5/5
Extremely relevant for software leads and PMs. It provides a disciplined, process-oriented framework that counters the "chaos" often found in modern agile environments by emphasizing accountability and formal change control.
