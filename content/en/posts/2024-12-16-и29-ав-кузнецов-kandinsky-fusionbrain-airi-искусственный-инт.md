## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko speaks with Andrey Kuznetsov, the head of the FusionBrain laboratory at the AIRI Institute and one of the lead developers of Sber's Kandinsky neural network. The conversation centers on the technical and philosophical challenges of building state-of-the-art generative models in Russia. Kuznetsov details the development of Kandinsky, explaining how their team managed to compete with global giants like OpenAI by focusing on architectural efficiency and multilingual support rather than just sheer computing power.

A major theme of the discussion is the "data ceiling." Kuznetsov notes that AI models have already consumed roughly 95% of high-quality human-generated data available on the internet. This leads to a critical turning point where future models will likely need to learn from synthetic data—robots teaching robots—or develop entirely new learning paradigms. Regarding the future of software engineering, Kuznetsov suggests that while AI won't replace programmers, it will fundamentally shift their role. Developers will move away from writing syntax and routine boilerplate, becoming "architects of meanings" who manage AI agents and oversee high-level system design.

The interview also touches on Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). While Kuznetsov believes AGI is achievable, he remains grounded, characterizing current Large Language Models (LLMs) as "stochastic parrots" that still lack a true understanding of causality. He emphasizes the importance of academic research and the role of institutes like AIRI in bridging the gap between theoretical breakthroughs and industrial applications.

## Key Insights
- "The programmer of the future is an architect of meanings, not someone who just knows language syntax."
- "We are approaching the ceiling of human-created data; soon, we will see robots training other robots."
- "AGI is possible, but current models are still 'stochastic parrots' lacking a deep grasp of cause-and-effect relationships."
- "Competitive AI development isn't just about massive hardware; it's about finding architectural shortcuts and data efficiency."
