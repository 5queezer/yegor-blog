## Summary
In this lecture, Yegor Bugayenko explores the critical discipline of Scope Management, which he defines as the process of ensuring a project includes all the work required, and *only* the work required, to complete the project successfully. He distinguishes between "Product Scope" (the features and functions of the product) and "Project Scope" (the work performed to deliver that product). 

The core of the discussion revolves around the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS). Yegor emphasizes that a WBS is not a list of tasks or actions, but a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team to accomplish the project objectives and create the required deliverables. He introduces the "100% Rule," stating that the WBS represents 100% of the work defined by the project scope and captures all deliverables—internal, external, and interim.

A significant portion of the lecture is dedicated to the "enemies" of scope management: Scope Creep and Gold Plating. Scope Creep refers to the uncontrolled expansion of product or project scope without adjustments to time, cost, and resources. Gold Plating, which Yegor describes as a "crime" often committed by developers, is the practice of adding extra features or "polishing" the product beyond the agreed-upon requirements. Both lead to project failure by consuming resources that weren't allocated. He concludes that a project manager's primary job is to protect the boundaries of the scope.

## Key Insights
1. "Scope Management is not about what we will do, but about defining the boundaries of what we will NOT do."
2. "Gold plating is a crime committed by developers who think they are doing a favor to the client, while they are actually stealing from the project's budget."
3. "The WBS is a hierarchy of deliverables, not a sequence of activities; if it's not in the WBS, it’s not in the project."
4. "Scope Creep is a sign of a weak project manager who cannot say 'no' to stakeholders without a formal change request."

## Relevance: 5/5
This is foundational for software engineering. Understanding that "extra features" are actually defects in management is a paradigm shift for many developers and leads to better team discipline and predictable delivery.
