## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko talks with Andrey Borodin, a long-time PostgreSQL contributor and engineer. The discussion navigates through the philosophy of database selection, the competitive landscape of open-source engines, and the unique development culture of PostgreSQL. Borodin emphasizes an engineering-first perspective: naming conventions (Postgres vs. Postgre-S) matter less than clear communication among team members. When comparing PostgreSQL to MySQL, he points out that while MySQL may have architectural merits, PostgreSQL has pulled ahead because it remains "truly free." This openness allows developers to "inflict usefulness" on the project without having to navigate the corporate gatekeeping of entities like Oracle.

A significant portion of the conversation focuses on the "prehistoric" development process of PostgreSQL, which still relies heavily on mailing lists (PGSQL Hackers) rather than modern platforms like GitHub. While Yegor critiques this as an archaic "Fidonet-like" experience lacking syntax highlighting and modern UI, Borodin defends it. He argues that tools are rarely the real bottleneck in engineering. For him, the mailing list forces a focus on deep semantic intent and the "why" behind the code. He views the commit messages and discussions as a window into the thought processes of world-class engineers, prioritizing logical correctness and long-term architectural stability over superficial developer experience.

## Key Insights
1. **Engineering Horizon:** Choose tools not just for today's prototype, but for tasks that "go beyond the horizon of decades or petabytes."
2. **True Open Source:** PostgreSQL’s success is attributed to its community-driven nature, where contributing a patch is about merit and negotiation with a community, rather than a single corporation's roadmap.
3. **Thought over Syntax:** In high-level systems programming, code is a "shallow thing"; the real value lies in the "commit message and the letter," which represent the depth of human thought and intent.
4. **Communication Pragmatism:** Use terminology that ensures you are understood without misinterpretation; technical purity is secondary to team synchronization.

This transcript is highly relevant for understanding the governance of successful, long-lived open-source projects and the "boring technology" mindset required for mission-critical infrastructure.
