## Summary
This interview features Irina Kraineva, a historian and curator of the digital archive of Academician Andrei Ershov, a pioneer of Soviet computer science. The discussion explores the origins of the Soviet IT industry and the pivotal role of the Siberian school of programming. A central theme is the systematic effort to digitize thousands of Ershov’s personal and professional documents, which provide a rare window into the internal struggles of early Soviet informatics.

The most controversial topic discussed is the creation of the ES EVM (Unified System of Electronic Computers) in the late 1960s. The Soviet leadership decided to abandon original, domestic computer architectures (like the BESM series) in favor of cloning the Western IBM System/360. Yegor and Irina discuss whether this was a "strategic diversion" or a fatal mistake that led to technological stagnation. By choosing to copy instead of innovate, the USSR became a "follower," losing its unique school of thought and its competitive edge in hardware and software design.

The interview also highlights Ershov’s vision of "programming as the second literacy," his advocacy for computer science in schools, and his role as a bridge between Soviet scientists and the international community. It serves as a cautionary tale about the long-term consequences of architectural decisions and the importance of maintaining technological sovereignty.

## Key Insights
- "Programming is the second literacy" — Andrei Ershov’s famous slogan that drove the introduction of informatics into the Soviet education system.
- The decision to clone the IBM 360 (ES EVM) was a turning point that shifted the USSR from a creator of unique architectures to a technological dependent.
- Historical archives are not just for historians; they are the "source code" of the industry’s identity and mistakes.
- Software engineering culture is deeply influenced by the hardware architectures it is built upon.
