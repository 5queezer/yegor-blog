## Summary
In this final lecture of the PMBA series, Yegor Bugayenko redefines stakeholder management through a lens of strict discipline and systemic boundaries. Departing from the "Agile" focus on soft skills and constant collaboration, Yegor argues that a Project Manager (PM) must act as a "lawmaker" and a "shield" for the technical team. The primary goal is to protect developers from the "noise" and chaotic whims of stakeholders—clients, sponsors, and users—who can disrupt productivity.

Yegor emphasizes that stakeholders should be managed via formal protocols, contracts, and clear reporting rather than personal relationships. He advocates for the "Power/Interest Grid" to prioritize communication and suggests "selective transparency." Instead of inviting stakeholders into every meeting, the PM should provide high-frequency, low-volume automated reports. This keeps stakeholders informed and satisfied without allowing them to micro-manage the development process. Ultimately, Yegor views stakeholder dissatisfaction not as a personal failure, but as a failure of the communication "law" or the underlying process, which must be fixed mechanically.

## Key Insights
- "The Project Manager is not a boss, but a designer of the environment and a buffer between the team and the chaos of the outside world."
- "Conflict with a stakeholder is a sign of a broken system or a missing rule, not a lack of charisma or 'soft skills'."
- "High-frequency, low-volume communication (like automated daily status updates) is the most effective way to keep stakeholders satisfied while keeping them at a distance."
- "Stakeholders are truly satisfied by results and delivered code, not by being invited to meetings where they don't belong."

## Relevance: 5/5
Extremely relevant for software engineers and team leads. It provides a pragmatic, albeit rigid, framework for protecting the engineering process from external interference and formalizing the "interface" between business and code.
