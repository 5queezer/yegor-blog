## Summary
In this interview, Professor Nelly Litvak discusses the evolving role of mathematics in the age of artificial intelligence. Contrary to the belief that AI might make math obsolete, she argues that its importance is actually growing. As our world becomes increasingly digital, mathematics remains the foundational science capable of modeling and understanding this data-driven reality. She compares the rise of AI to the historical introduction of computers: while automation handles the "low-level" calculations, it simultaneously shifts the human role toward higher-level abstraction, system modeling, and problem-solving.

A significant portion of the discussion focuses on the future of education. Litvak is skeptical that AI or platforms like YouTube will replace traditional universities. She defines "true education" as a process of cognitive development that requires a structured "safe space" and, most importantly, timely feedback. Learning is not just about consuming information but about active engagement and "intellectual load." She highlights that humans have an inherent biological need for mental challenges; without them, individuals often face boredom and depression. Furthermore, she advocates for modern pedagogical techniques such as active learning and spaced repetition (returning to material at intervals) to ensure long-term knowledge retention. For young professionals, her advice is to seek out intellectual complexity, as the ability to create abstract models of real-world systems is the skill least likely to be replicated by AI.

## Key Insights
* "Mathematics is the only science capable of processing and making sense of our digital world."
* "The essence of education is receiving feedback while there is still time to correct your mistakes."
* "Humans have a biological necessity for intellectual load; without it, we fall into boredom and depression."
* "AI doesn't replace mathematicians; it shifts their work from calculation to higher-level modeling, much like computers did decades ago."

## Relevance: 4/5
This discussion is highly relevant to software engineering as it emphasizes the importance of abstract modeling and feedback loops—concepts central to system design and agile management.
