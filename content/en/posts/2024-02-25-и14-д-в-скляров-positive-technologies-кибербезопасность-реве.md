## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko speaks with Dmitry Sklyarov, a legendary figure in the cybersecurity world and the Head of Reverse Engineering at Positive Technologies. The conversation delves into the philosophy of security, beginning with a look back at Sklyarov's 2001 arrest in the United States—a landmark legal case involving the DMCA and Adobe’s eBook protection. Sklyarov uses this historical context to explain how the industry has shifted from amateur curiosity to professionalized, high-stakes defense and offense.

A central theme is the art and science of reverse engineering. Sklyarov defines it not as a tool for "breaking" things, but as a fundamental engineering process used to reconstruct the lost intent of a developer when the source code is unavailable. He argues that a deep understanding of low-level systems, machine code, and memory management is essential for building truly secure software. He strongly critiques the concept of "security by obscurity," asserting that any system whose safety depends on its inner workings remaining a secret is fundamentally broken.

For software engineers, the discussion provides a masterclass in the adversarial mindset. Sklyarov emphasizes that security must be an intrinsic property of a system’s architecture, not an afterthought or a "patch" applied later. He encourages developers to move beyond high-level abstractions and understand the underlying hardware to prevent trivial but catastrophic vulnerabilities. The interview concludes with insights into the future of the field, highlighting that while AI and automation are helpful, human intuition and the ability to "think like a hacker" remain irreplaceable.

## Key Insights
1. "Reverse engineering is the process of recovering the knowledge and intent that were lost during the compilation of a program."
2. "Security is not a product or a feature; it is a fundamental property of a system that must be designed into its DNA from the start."
3. "Security by obscurity is a fallacy; a truly secure system remains safe even when the attacker knows exactly how it works."
4. "The most dangerous vulnerabilities often hide in the logic that everyone assumes to be correct because they never bothered to verify it at a low level."

## Relevance: 5/5
This interview is highly relevant for software engineers and architects as it challenges the reliance on abstractions and highlights the critical importance of secure-by-design principles in modern software development.
