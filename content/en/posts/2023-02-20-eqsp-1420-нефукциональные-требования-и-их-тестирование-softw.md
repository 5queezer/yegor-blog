## Summary
In this lecture, Yegor Bugayenko explores the critical boundary between functional and non-functional requirements (NFRs). He argues that while the customer defines the business logic (functional requirements), the developer or architect is solely responsible for the technical quality of the system (NFRs). The core message is that technical excellence—such as performance, scalability, security, and thread safety—cannot be offloaded to the customer; it is the professional duty of the engineer to ensure these are met.

Bugayenko emphasizes that NFRs must be measurable and integrated into the automated CI/CD pipeline. He categorizes testing into performance testing (measuring speed under normal conditions) and stress testing (finding the breaking point). Using a medical analogy, he likens unit tests to blood work and NFR testing to a marathon endurance test: a patient might have healthy organs individually, but that doesn't mean they can survive a strenuous physical challenge. A significant part of the lecture is dedicated to thread safety in Java as a prime example of an NFR that often fails under concurrency if not properly designed from the start. Ultimately, the software expert must impose high quality standards because they, not the customer, understand the technical risks.

## Key Insights
1. "The customer is responsible for the business result, while the programmer is responsible for technical quality."
2. "Non-functional requirements must be part of CI/CD, not checked once a year before release."
3. "NFR testing is like checking the system's endurance—whether it can run a marathon."
4. "A good architect lays NFRs into the foundation; a poor one hopes for the best until problems arise."
