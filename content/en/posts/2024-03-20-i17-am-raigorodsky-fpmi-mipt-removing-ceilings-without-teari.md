## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko talks with Andrei Mikhailovich Raigorodsky, the Director of the Phystech School of Applied Mathematics and Informatics (FPMI) at MIPT. The discussion explores the delicate balance between maintaining a rigorous academic foundation and fostering an environment for explosive professional growth. Raigorodsky presents his philosophy of "removing ceilings"—providing students with unlimited opportunities for high-level achievement—while "not tearing off the roof," which refers to preserving the traditional, intensive "Phystech" system of fundamental education.

A central theme is the critical role of deep mathematics in modern software engineering. Raigorodsky argues that while basic coding might not require advanced math, creating breakthrough technologies in fields like AI, cryptography, and network optimization is impossible without expertise in Graph Theory, Combinatorics, and Group Theory. He views mathematics not just as a tool, but as a "humanitarian" science that develops abstract thinking and the ability to find patterns across disparate domains. The interview also touches on the successful integration of industry giants (like Yandex and Sber) into the academic ecosystem, where corporate-funded labs solve real-world problems without compromising scientific integrity. Ultimately, Raigorodsky warns against "superficial engineering," advocating for specialists who understand the "why" behind their tools to avoid becoming mere operators of complex machinery they don't comprehend.

## Key Insights
- "Removing ceilings without tearing off the roof": A strategy to provide unlimited growth opportunities while maintaining a strict, foundational academic structure.
- "Mathematics is a humanitarian science": Math is about the human ability to connect abstract facts and find hidden structures, much like historical analysis.
- "Monkeys flying an aircraft": A metaphor for developers who use powerful frameworks and complex tools without understanding the underlying mathematical and algorithmic principles.
- The "Phystech" ecosystem thrives on "industrial-academic synthesis," where industry funding supports fundamental research that later feeds back into breakthrough commercial products.

## Relevance: 5/5
This interview is highly relevant for software engineers and architects as it highlights the necessity of foundational theoretical knowledge (Discrete Math, Algorithms) for solving complex, non-trivial engineering problems and avoiding the trap of "commodity" coding.
