## Summary
In this episode, Yegor analyzes a variety of recent tech news topics, focusing heavily on their underlying implications for software engineering and team management. He highlights Pavel Durov's recent interview, pointing out that Telegram’s ability to serve nearly a billion users with a surprisingly small team of engineers is proof that compact, highly competent teams outperform massive, bloated corporate structures. The episode critiques the regulatory struggles involving TikTok, Apple, and the FCC, viewing them as political maneuvers and bureaucratic overhead rather than genuine technical or user-protection milestones. Furthermore, Yegor discusses the AI hardware hype, particularly the Rabbit R1 and Meta's LLAMA-3 release, expressing skepticism about the rush to market without solid, maintainable software architecture. Finally, he briefly reviews KasperskyOS, praising its strict microkernel architecture and security-by-design principles as a refreshing contrast to typically bloated modern operating systems.

## Key Insights
- "Telegram's engineering structure, with only a few dozen developers, is a masterclass in eliminating management bloat and focusing on technical excellence."
- "The hype around AI hardware like Rabbit R1 often ignores the core software engineering principles required to make such products sustainable and truly useful."
- "KasperskyOS provides a fascinating study in microkernel architecture and applying strict structural boundaries for security-by-design."

## Relevance: 4/5
Highly relevant for engineering managers and software architects. It directly contrasts bloated corporate structures with highly efficient, small technical teams, and touches on fundamental principles of system architecture and maintainability.
