## Summary
In this interview, Yegor Bugayenko talks with Anton Korobeynikov, a prominent contributor to the LLVM project. The discussion centers on the evolution of compiler technology, specifically focusing on how LLVM transformed from a research project into the industry standard. Korobeynikov explains that LLVM's primary value lies in its Intermediate Representation (IR) and modular framework, which allows developers to build new languages without reinventing optimization and code generation logic.

The conversation delves into the historical competition with GCC, highlighting the architectural and philosophical differences that led to LLVM's widespread adoption by tech giants. A significant portion of the interview is dedicated to MLIR (Multi-Level Intermediate Representation), which Korobeynikov describes as the solution to the "aging" of the original LLVM IR. MLIR enables hierarchical representations necessary for modern AI/ML workloads and heterogeneous computing (GPUs, TPUs).

Beyond systems programming, Korobeynikov shares his experience in bioinformatics. He explains how Computer Science methods are applied to analyze complex biological systems, such as the soil microbiome. He discusses a fascinating study on plant gigantism in the taiga, demonstrating how data analysis can reveal the microbial factors behind agricultural yields. The interview concludes with advice for junior developers, emphasizing that the compiler industry is currently experiencing a "renaissance" due to the demand for specialized hardware and AI-specific optimizations.

## Key Insights
- "LLVM is not just a set of tools; it is primarily a modular framework and an Intermediate Representation that redefined how we build compilers."
- "MLIR is the modern evolution required to handle high-level abstractions for AI/ML, which the original LLVM IR was never designed to represent efficiently."
- "The demand for compiler engineers has skyrocketed because software must now be surgically optimized for diverse, specialized hardware like NPUs and TPUs."
- "Bioinformatics isn't just biology; it's the application of heavy Computer Science to decode mysteries like why certain soil microbes cause plant gigantism."
