## Summary
In this insightful discussion, Yegor Bugayenko interviews Anatoly T. Fomenko, the primary architect of the "New Chronology" (NC) theory. The conversation pivots away from traditional historical debate and focuses on history as a problem of applied mathematics and data verification. Fomenko argues that the standard chronological timeline (Scaligerian) was constructed in the 17th century with political biases and is riddled with mathematical inconsistencies. He explains how he uses statistical analysis of historical texts and astronomical dating of ancient zodiacs and eclipses to reveal "duplicates"—historical figures and events that were supposedly different but are mathematically identical in description and timing, suggesting they are reflections of the same medieval events shifted back in time.

Yegor explores the intersection of this theory with modern technology, asking how software engineering, AI, and big data could be used to verify or debunk these claims. Fomenko welcomes the use of AI to process vast historical archives, emphasizing that the calculation of dates should be a neutral, scientific process rather than a narrative-driven one. They discuss the "Dark Ages" as a chronological vacuum and how the NC theory inadvertently "equalizes" civilizations by showing that many "ancient" cultures are closer in time and development than previously thought. The interview highlights Fomenko's stoic approach to academic criticism, prioritizing mathematical provability over consensus.

## Key Insights
- "History is not just a narrative; it is a branch of applied mathematics where dates must be calculated, not assigned by consensus."
- The "Dark Ages" are essentially a "chronological shift," a vacuum created by projecting medieval history thousands of years into the past.
- "Mathematical dating is inherently neutral; it lacks the nationalist or political bias that often plagues traditional historical writing."
- Modern software and AI are the next logical steps for historical verification, turning history into a searchable, verifiable database.

## Relevance: 4/5
The discussion is highly relevant to software engineers as it treats history like "legacy code" that requires refactoring. The concept of "duplicates" mirrors the identification of duplicate logic in codebases, and Fomenko’s insistence on mathematical verifiability over "storytelling" aligns perfectly with the principles of unit testing and formal verification in software development.
