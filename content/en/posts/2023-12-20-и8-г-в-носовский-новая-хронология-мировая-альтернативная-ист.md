## Summary
In this thought-provoking interview, Yegor Bugayenko speaks with Gleb Vladimirovich Nosovsky, a prominent Russian mathematician and co-author of the highly controversial "New Chronology" theory alongside Anatoly Fomenko. Nosovsky thoroughly explains the fundamental principles of the New Chronology, asserting that world history is significantly shorter than traditionally accepted. He argues that ancient historical timelines have been artificially extended through the phantom duplication of events, misdated records, and mistranslations over centuries. He elaborates on how their team applied strict mathematical, statistical, and astronomical methods to analyze historical chronicles, ultimately concluding that many pivotal events traditionally attributed to Antiquity actually occurred during the Middle Ages.

The discussion also delves deeply into the intense criticism and widespread accusations of pseudoscience that the theory faces from the mainstream academic community. Nosovsky vigorously defends their mathematical methodology against various efforts to "debunk" it, emphasizing the critical importance of questioning established dogmas and relying on verifiable mathematical proofs rather than blindly accepting historical consensus. Throughout the conversation, Yegor Bugayenko probes the logical foundations of these claims, exploring how an analytical mind approaches systemic flaws in deeply entrenched narratives. For software engineers and technical leaders, the interview serves as an abstract but fascinating exploration of paradigm shifts, the extreme difficulty of challenging established systems of thought, and the application of rigorous, data-driven analysis to subjective, poorly documented, or chaotically structured fields of information.

## Key Insights
- Mathematical methods and data analysis can be used to identify anomalies and phantom duplications in large datasets, radically challenging established timelines.
- An established scientific or historical consensus can sometimes function more like a dogma, becoming heavily resistant to paradigm-shifting discoveries.
- Analyzing historical narratives reveals how data can be manipulated, duplicated, or misinterpeted over centuries, emphasizing the need for independent, empirical verification.
